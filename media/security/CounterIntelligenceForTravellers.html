Counterintelligence Travel Tips

Protect What You Know

Why Are You a Target?

Many foreign governments and businesses place a high priority on acquiring U.S. Government (USG) information. They target USG travelers or even family members to get to it.

Intelligence collectors seek:

  • U.S. political, economic, industrial, and military information.
  • Classified and sensitive government information.
  • Proprietary company data.

You may not know what information they are looking for, that helps them identify and pursue even more lucrative targets.

Foreign intelligence collectors hope to acquire information surreptitiously and usually do not draw attention to the tactics they use.

Only rarely have foreign intelligence collectors stolen a laptop, directly recruited someone, or trapped someone in an embarrassing act. USG travelers have reported incidents from all over the world. International borders do not restrict foreign intelligence collection.

Who else can target you for intelligence collection?

  • A third country, while the host country is unaware.
  • Foreign government ministries unassociated with a country’s intelligence or security organizations.
  • Foreign research institutes.
  • Government-sponsored industries.
  • Terrorist organizations.

How Is Intelligence Gathered?

Elicitation

Seemingly normal conversations contrived to get information about you, your work, or your colleagues. This is subtle and is not direct questioning by government or industry representatives. These methods are designed to:

  • Put you at ease to share information.
  • Be difficult to recognize as intelligence collection.
  • Be easily deniable by an adversary if confronted.
For example, an intelligence gatherer posing as a scientist at a conference who may even be credentialed in your field of expertise may engage you in conversation to get information from you.

Electronic Interception

Legal or illegal intercept of electronic communication such as cell phones or Bluetooth devices. Computers and miniature electronic surveillance equipment are low-cost, effective tools to find out more about you than you want to share.

  • Foreign telecommunications carriers and internet service providers are vulnerable because many are government controlled.
  • Office, hotel, and cell phones are key targets.
  • Fax machines and e-mail may be electronically monitored.

Evesdropping

Listening to other people’s conversations to gather information. This can range from strategic positioning of an unobtrusive bystander to using concealed audio and visual devices. Eavesdropping can be used in social settings, public and host-provided transport, restaurants, bars, and meeting facility restrooms where you feel secure and are more likely to talk about yourself or your work.

Concealed listening and recording devices can also be placed in conference facilities and hotel rooms to overhear or record conversations. These are low cost, low risk, and virtually undetectable.

Intrusion

Physical entry into a room, briefcase, room safe, or electronic equipment to steal or copy papers, magnetic and audio media, or install electronic eavesdropping equipment. Most intrusions are surreptitious but travelers have reported finding someone searching their belongings or conducting unnecessary maintenance.

  • Hotel intrusions are often done with help from hotel staff.
  • When reported, authorities may try to explain away incidents as criminal activity, for example, someone seeking passports, cash, and valuables.
  • Most countries and foreign companies can override commercial computer intrusion-protection software and hardware or break commercially available encryption.

How Can You Protect Yourself?

Awareness, common sense, and basic personal security measures can help to protect you from attempts to collect information. Here are some things you can do before, during, and after your trip:

Get Threat Information Before You Go

Be sure you know your program’s foreign travel procedures and get foreign intelligence threat information.

Keep a Low Profile

Your behavior overseas could increase your visibility and susceptibility to compromise or loss of information. In some cases, it may be hard to maintain a low profile. The nature of your travel, such as attending a conference, could associate you with a government program or project. Even the clothes you wear may draw attention.

  • Unless required to do so, do not wear clothing with a USG or program logo in public. This also reduces your chance of becoming a victim of criminal activity, civil unrest, or terrorism.
  • Avoid using commercial or government encryption programs for e-mails. Some countries may interpret encrypted e-mail as being sent by a terrorist, drug trafficker, or criminal.
  • Avoid associating with people labelled by the USG as dissidents and steer clear of demonstrations.

Be Reserved in Sharing Information

Unless dealing with security authorities in the host country, such as border police or customs officials, avoid providing information that identifies you as a USG traveler.

  • Provide travel arrangers, drivers, and hotel staff with only the information necessary to conduct your transactions.
  • Do not offer information to anyone not authorized to hear it, including facts about the USG, the agency, your work, or personal information about yourself or colleagues.
  • Ignore or deflect unwarranted inquiries or intrusive conversations.
  • Do not discuss classified, sensitive, or proprietary information on public transport, in hotel rooms, or other public venues.
  • Do not leave classified or sensitive documents or equipment unattended in hotel rooms or hotel safes. Use only approved security devices for classified or proprietary information.
  • Do not use foreign computers, faxes, or telephones to transmit or discuss classified or sensitive company information.

    • Report Incidents

    Suspicious incidents should be reported to the nearest U.S. diplomatic authorities.

    • When in doubt, seek assistance from appropriate local US government security representatives or your company.
    • Do not hesitate to report seemingly minor incidents.
